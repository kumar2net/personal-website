## What the science actually agrees on

Three points are broadly uncontested:

1. **Plastics are everywhere** â€” in air, food systems, water, clothing, and packaging.
2. **Humans are exposed daily**, especially through indoor environments.
3. **Measuring tiny particles inside the body is extremely difficult**, and some high-profile studies may overstate certainty due to methodological limits.

In other words:  
Exposure is real.  
Health impacts are still being worked out.  
Waiting for perfect certainty is not how public health works.

## The public-health lens (why action still makes sense)

Public health does not wait for 100% proof.

It acts when three conditions are met:

- The risk is *plausible*
- The intervention is *low-cost*
- The downside of action is *minimal*

Microplastics now fall squarely into this category.

The goal is **risk reduction**, not elimination.

## Where exposure most likely comes from

Contrary to popular belief, food is not the only â€” or even the dominant â€” route.

Current evidence points to three major pathways:

### 1. Indoor air and dust
Synthetic clothes, carpets, furniture, and household dust shed microscopic plastic fibres that are inhaled over time.

### 2. Heat + plastic contact
Heating food or liquids in plastic containers significantly increases particle shedding.

### 3. Water systems and packaging
Bottled water and poorly filtered systems can contribute more plastic than many people assume.

The podcast strongly emphasises that **inhalation and everyday contact matter at least as much as diet**.

## What actually helps (without going extreme)

### Tier 1: Strongly recommended (high impact, low effort)

These are sensible defaults, not lifestyle overhauls.

- Avoid heating food in plastic containers
- Use glass or steel for hot drinks
- Reduce reliance on single-use bottled water
- Ventilate indoor spaces regularly
- Wet-clean floors and surfaces to reduce dust

These actions target exposure routes that are well-established, even if long-term health effects are still being quantified.

---

### Tier 2: Reasonable risk reduction (optional, context-dependent)

- Use a basic carbon or RO water filter
- Wash synthetic clothes less frequently and on gentle cycles
- Prefer natural fibres for everyday, close-to-skin wear when practical
- Consider HEPA air filtration for sleeping spaces if affordable

Helpful, but not mandatory. No need for perfection.
